# Vision — Acme Shop

## Problem Narrative

Boutique fashion brands have two bad options. They can sell on a marketplace (Etsy, an Instagram shop bolted onto a
generic checkout) where 30%+ of every sale evaporates in fees and commissions, the brand's name is buried under
algorithmic competitors, and the marketplace — not the brand — owns the customer relationship. Or they can stand up a
generic storefront (Shopify, Big Cartel) that keeps more of the revenue but treats a fashion label exactly like a
hardware store: there is no notion of a "fan", no way to message the 200 people who bought last season's drop, and no
loyalty mechanic beyond a coupon code.

The brands we serve are small — typically one owner plus one to three staff — and their economics live or die on
repeat purchases from a devoted base of fans, not on one-time impulse buys from strangers. Yet the tools available to
them are optimized for exactly the opposite: maximizing first-time conversion from anonymous traffic. The fan
relationship, which is the brand's single most valuable asset, is left on the table.

Acme Shop closes that gap. We give a boutique brand a storefront that keeps the revenue *and* keeps the fan
relationship — the direct line to the people who already love the brand.

## Personas

- **shopper** — a returning fan of one or a few small brands. Discovers a brand through Instagram, a friend, or a
  previous purchase; comes back for new drops and restocks. Values the direct connection to the maker and is willing to
  pay a premium over a marketplace price because they want the brand to thrive. Shops mostly on mobile.
- **brand-owner** — owns and runs a single boutique label. Often the designer, marketer, and CEO in one person. Makes
  every commercial decision: pricing, which platform to sell on, how much revenue they keep. Wants a low-overhead
  storefront, a high revenue-keep, and — increasingly — a way to talk directly to fans without renting an audience from
  a social platform.
- **store-admin** — a brand's day-to-day operator (the owner's one to three staff). Lives in the catalog, inventory,
  and order queue. Cares about how fast they can publish a new product, whether stock counts stay accurate during a
  drop, and how little the tool gets in their way.
- **ops-manager** — Acme internal. Owns the brand-onboarding pipeline and payout operations. Measured on how quickly a
  new brand goes live and on keeping payout latency low and payout errors near zero.
- **support-agent** — Acme internal. Handles both shopper-side tickets (where is my order) and brand-side tickets
  (a payout looks wrong, a product won't publish). Needs the order, payout, and catalog state to be legible.

## Value Proposition

For boutique brands, Acme Shop is the only storefront that lets them keep ~95% of revenue **and** own a direct
fan relationship — the messaging, segments, and repeat-purchase loyalty that marketplaces deliberately withhold.

## North-Star

Repeat-purchase rate per brand. Every other metric (GMV, brands onboarded, payout latency) is a means; the brand's
ability to turn a buyer into a repeat fan is the end. If a brand's fans come back, the brand thrives and stays on Acme.

## 1–3 Year Direction

**Year 1 (done, 2024–25):** Ship the core storefront — catalog, single-page checkout, automated brand payouts.
Onboard the first 100 brands and prove the direct-to-fan economics. _Achieved: 120+ brands, ~$2.4M GMV, sub-3-day
payouts._

**Year 2 (in progress, 2025–26):** Build the differentiator — fan-CRM (broadcast messaging, reply inbox, audience
segments) so brands can actually use the fan relationship we promised them — plus a brand analytics dashboard, and
begin native mobile.

**Year 3 (planned):** Native mobile shopping at scale and cross-brand discovery — letting a fan of one Acme brand find
adjacent brands — *without* degrading into a marketplace that re-commoditizes the brands or taxes them on fees.

## Principles

- The fan relationship belongs to the brand, never to Acme.
- Keep the brand's revenue with the brand: low, transparent fees, no surprise commissions.
- Optimize for repeat purchase, not first-click conversion.
- Boring, reliable money movement: payouts are fast, correct, and predictable.

## Non-Goals

- We are not a marketplace and will not rank brands against each other for impressions.
- We do not monetize the fan list or sell shopper data.
- We are not a general-purpose e-commerce platform for every vertical — fashion brands with a fan base are the focus.

## Differentiation

Shopify and Big Cartel give a brand a checkout but no fan layer. Etsy gives a fan layer (sort of) but takes the
relationship and the margin. Substack proved fans will pay creators directly but has no commerce/inventory spine. Acme
is the intersection no incumbent occupies: a real storefront with real inventory and payouts, fronted by a fan-CRM that
treats the audience as the brand's own.
